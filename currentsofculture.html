Where the Columbia River Divides

Christina Chahrouri

Content Map

17" x 22"

Representing the project’s analytical and

confrontational core, the Columbia River Map

serves to deconstruct the complex

intersection of colonial law, indigenous

sovereignty, and environmental management.


Its brand identity is defined by a "System

Map" aesthetic, using a rigid, clean grid to

represent Euro-American structuralism and

bureaucratic history.



Simultaneously, it allows indigenous creation

myths and linguistic features to occupy the

lower "flow" of the layout, symbolizing the

foundational and enduring nature of ancestral

knowledge.


This piece promises a "whole-truth"

perspective on land use, challenging the

viewer to see the map not as a tool of

ownership, but as a vital instrument for

reconciliation.

Spread of Arabic along the Nile Christina Chahrouri Brochure 8" x 10" vertical

This brochure functions as the project's intellectual foundation, adopting a tone that is academic yet deeply interpretive. Its mission is to demonstrate how the physical geography of the Nile dictated the evolution of human thought and speech over millennia. A major influence within the branding is Arabic typography and calligraphic structure. Arabic script's flowing, connected letterforms reflect the organic movement of rivers, creating a visual parallel between written language and natural currents. By utilizing a color palette of desert sand and deep teal, this piece connects the landscape of the river to its rich linguistic culture

Mankind VS. Mother Nature Christina Chahrouri installation 20 feet

This installation moves the project into the physical realm, adopting a tone that is monumental and deeply reflective. This sculpture designed to force a literal encounter with the scale of our environmental footprint, serving as a permanent monument to the idea that nature is not a place we visit, but a system we inhabit. Mankind VS. Mother Nature reimagines the pinnacle of human achievement through the lens of our elemental connection to the earth. By utilizing heroic representations of the human sculpture a motif long revered as a cultural cornerstone in art, the piece elevates the human form to a monumental status. However, this is not a celebration of dominance, but a study in relationship. The "vein-mapping" on the sculpture serves as a graphic overlay on the physical world, using the changing water levels as a dynamic part of the data. It promises to turn the landscape itself into a gallery, inviting the viewer to participate in a silent, powerful dialogue about human ambition and natural resilience
